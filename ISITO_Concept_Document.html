// 01

Executive Summary

ISITO is a permanently crewed orbital infrastructure platform designed to grow with humanity's ambitions over multiple generations. It is not a destination — it is a foundation. Phase 1 establishes a crewed habitat, deep-space communications hub, and orbital servicing depot in Low Earth Orbit. Each subsequent phase expands capability, crew capacity, and industrial output as consortium membership and technology mature together.

The project is structured from day one to generate operational revenue — not in some distant future when the shipyard is complete, but immediately, through communications leasing, orbital repair services, and research contracts. This is a self-sustaining industrial platform, not a science experiment.

// Primary Mission

Communications Nexus

Deep-space relay, Earth-to-orbit coordination, AI-assisted traffic management. Fills real gaps in current DSN coverage. Generates revenue from day one of operations with existing customers.

// Secondary Mission

Permanent Human Habitat

12-person rotating crew on 6–12 month tours. Artificial gravity ring at 0.3–0.5g. Full life support, medical bay, circadian lighting. A place humans can actually live and work.

// Tertiary Mission

Repair, Refuel & Build

Orbital servicing depot for current commercial vehicles. Crew Return Vehicle as first orbital shipbuilding program. The proving ground for humanity's first true space shipyard.

NASA CLD Alignment: NASA's Commercial Low Earth Orbit Destinations (CLD) program is actively awarding contracts to exactly this class of platform. Axiom Space received $140M. Starlab (Voyager/Nanoracks) is funded. ISITO's Phase 1 design is structurally eligible and should be positioned to compete for or formally partner with CLD funding before any private capital round.

Orbital location is not a preference — it is an engineering decision that drives every other subsystem. The phased migration strategy below ensures Phase 1 uses proven, accessible infrastructure while preserving the long-term deep-space mission geometry.

ARTIFICIAL GRAVITY — ROTATION SPECIFICATIONS

There is no naturally occurring "zero-g zone." Microgravity exists everywhere in freefall orbit. The habitat ring generates artificial gravity purely through centripetal acceleration. Phase 1 targets 0.3–0.5g — sufficient to prevent critical bone and muscle degradation, buildable at Phase 1 scale, and a proven concept in theoretical and simulation studies.

Power Architecture

A fusion reactor is not in scope for any phase within a credible engineering timeline. The commercially available fusion power that would be needed does not exist and is not funded for production within this century's first half. The power roadmap below uses proven and near-term technology at each phase, scaling to fission as NASA's Kilopower and Fission Surface Power programs mature.

Why Not Fusion: ITER, the world's largest fusion experiment, will not produce net power output — it is a science instrument. Private fusion companies (Helion, Commonwealth Fusion) target demonstration in the late 2020s, but no compact space-deployable fusion power system exists, is funded, or has a credible production timeline within Phase 1 or 2. Fission is the correct bridge technology.

Critical advantage of fission supplementation: eclipse periods in LEO are approximately 35–40 minutes per 90-minute orbit. Solar alone cannot guarantee 24/7 power for critical life support and communications systems without very large battery banks. Even 2 Kilopower units provide eclipse-proof baseline power for life support and comms at minimal mass cost.

RADIATION SHIELDING STRATEGY

// Primary Shield

Water Wall Panels

Water is one of the best radiation shielding materials per unit mass and serves dual purpose as emergency drinking water reserve. Integrated into habitat ring hull panels. Target ~10 cm water equivalent. Mass cost: ~30 MT for Phase 1 habitat coverage.

// Secondary Shield

Polyethylene Composites

High hydrogen content makes polyethylene highly effective against GCR (Galactic Cosmic Rays). Used in sleeping quarters and medical bay panels where crew spend most stationary time. Lighter than water equivalent per unit shielding at thickness constraints.

// Storm Shelter

Solar Particle Event Bunker

A dedicated heavily-shielded "storm shelter" module — approximately 20 m³ — where full crew can shelter during Solar Particle Events (SPEs). SPEs can deliver lethal doses in hours without shielding. Storm shelter must be reachable from any point on the station in under 5 minutes.

// 06

Crew Return Vehicle — First Build

The Crew Return Vehicle program is simultaneously ISITO's emergency escape system and its first orbital shipbuilding proof-of-concept. This sequencing is intentional — the station needs this vehicle on day one of crewed operations regardless of the shipbuilding mission. The first CRV has a guaranteed customer before a single subassembly is manufactured.

Why this is the right first build: You are not trying to build a starship in orbit. You are proving that orbital fabrication and assembly is possible using a vehicle small enough that its first failure is survivable data — not a program-ending catastrophe. Every CRV built generates on-orbit manufacturing heritage. Heritage justifies the next vehicle. That is how you build a shipyard program.

01

Earth Manufacture of Subassemblies

Pressure vessel sections, heat shield panels, avionics bay, parachute system, RCS thruster quads manufactured to SIS tolerances. No single assembly exceeds Starship cargo bay volume constraints.

02

Launch as Cargo — Not as a Vehicle

Subassemblies fly as cargo on multiple Starship flights. This is the core of the proof-of-concept — you are building a spacecraft from parts that were never assembled on Earth.

03

On-Orbit Assembly at MOD-06 Bay

Station robotic arms and EVA crew assemble sections in the CRV Construction Bay. Pressure testing, leak checks, systems integration conducted in orbit.

04

CRV Docks as Emergency Vehicle

Certified CRV docks to station and serves as primary crew emergency return capability. This is the station's most critical safety asset.

05

Operational Reentry After 180 Days

CRV undocks, performs autonomous deorbit burn, ocean splashdown. Recovery data — thermal performance, structural behavior, systems reliability — feeds directly into CRV #2 design.

06

Iterate — This Is Your Shipbuilding Program

CRV #2 incorporates lessons learned. CRV #3 adds improvements. By CRV #4 you have genuine orbital manufacturing heritage. That heritage is the engineering foundation for the Orbital Transfer Vehicle program and eventually the full shipyard.

Governance & Legal Framework

ISITO operates under Model B consortium equity governance — proportional equity stakes tied to investment value, with non-negotiable SpaceX/NASA technical authority at the SIS level. The closest real-world analog is CERN, where member nations hold proportional stakes but the Director-General and Scientific Council retain final authority over safety and operational decisions regardless of member preferences.

Model B — Proportional Consortium Equity

Investment value determines equity percentage. Equity holders vote on governance decisions proportionally. Revenue distributed as dividends after operational costs. SpaceX/NASA hold founding equity plus permanent SIS authority regardless of future dilution.

// Legal Note

Securities Registration Required

Model B equity issuance constitutes a security under US law (SEC jurisdiction) and international equivalents. Legal entity must be formally constituted before accepting investment. Securities attorney required before first term sheet. This is Phase 0 work — not optional.

// Station Name

Global Public Naming Campaign

The station's common name is determined by global public vote — not by any single company, nation, or investor. The formal treaty body name is ISITO. The station designation is determined by humanity. See Section 11 for campaign design.

SIS — Station Interface Standard: Every module contributed by any consortium member must comply with SIS before certification. SIS governs structural docking geometry and load ratings (IDSS foundation), power voltage and connector standards, atmospheric pressure and gas composition specs, data network protocol and bandwidth allocation, thermal rejection handoff requirements, and emergency bulkhead isolation capability. Non-compliance voids module certification. The SIS Technical Board (SpaceX + NASA) has unilateral authority to reject or de-certify any module. This is non-negotiable.

ISITO must generate operational revenue before the shipyard program produces a single vessel. The following revenue streams are active in Phase 1 — not contingent on Phase 3 shipyard completion. This is a critical investor credibility point.

NASA is actively awarding Commercial Low Earth Orbit Destinations contracts. ISITO Phase 1 is structurally eligible. Axiom received $140M. Target: $200–500M CLD contract as Phase 1 anchor funding.

Bandwidth leasing to lunar and Mars mission operators, NASA, ESA, and commercial deep-space companies. No equivalent service currently exists in orbit. Near-monopoly position in Phase 1.

// Phase 1 — Operational

Orbital Repair & Refueling

Every commercial satellite operator and launch provider is a potential paying client. Propellant depot services for visiting vehicles. Robotic repair of commercial satellites.

// Phase 1 — Operational

R&D Module Lease

ISS National Lab generates ~$150M/year. Rack space leased to pharma, materials science, universities, government agencies. Revenue independent of space industry familiarity.

// Phase 1 — Consortium

Module Slot Licensing

Companies pay for the right to design and attach a SIS-certified module. Module operational rights revert to consortium after defined period (Model A mechanics applied per module within Model B structure).

// Phase 1–2

CRV Construction Contracts

CRV program funded partly by NASA emergency vehicle contract, partly by sovereign space agencies needing crew return capability. Every CRV built funds the next iteration and the shipbuilding program.

What the Comms Hub Cannot Claim: ISITO's communications node cannot replace global satellite constellations like Starlink, OneWeb, or Kuiper. One orbital station has one ground footprint at a time — global coverage requires a constellation. ISITO's comms value is in deep-space relay, inter-vessel coordination, and emergency backbone — gaps that constellations do not fill. This distinction must be precise in all investor materials.

Design Language

The aesthetic direction for ISITO synthesizes two visual vocabularies: the clean, inhabited warmth of The Orville's Union-class architecture and the honest industrial cyberpunk of deep-space working environments. This is not purely cosmetic — the distinction between habitat zones and working zones maps directly to human factors engineering principles for long-duration crew performance and mental health.

HABITAT ZONES — The Orville Influence

Curved composite hull panels in matte warm-white and brushed metal. No sharp corners in living areas.

Circadian-cycling warm lighting (2700–3000K ambient). Crew health requirement doubles as aesthetic choice.

Generous viewport integration. Natural orbital light treated as a primary design element, not an afterthought.

Visual hierarchy by function — crew can read a space's purpose immediately without signage.

Lived-in quality. Not clinical. Evidence of habitation. Plants if bioreactor life support permits.

WORKING ZONES — Cyberpunk Industrial

Exposed structural truss members and organized utility conduit runs. Honest about what the station is made of.

High-contrast task lighting against darker ambient. Status indicator arrays visible at a glance.

Layered, additive construction aesthetic. Each new module visibly added to the existing structure.

Grip-textured modular floor panels. Replaceable units — maintenance access is part of the design.

Multilingual functional signage and digital status boards as visual elements, not intrusions.

This distinction is not arbitrary: Long-duration crew performance research consistently shows that environments which clearly delineate rest space from work space reduce psychological fatigue and improve decision-making quality over 6–12 month tours. The aesthetic choice is simultaneously a human factors engineering requirement. Every design decision in the habitat ring should be defensible on both grounds.

Global Naming Campaign

The station's common name is determined by a global public vote. This is not a marketing exercise — it is a deliberate act of shared ownership. Every person who participates feels personal stake in the mission. That is not sentiment. That is a legitimacy engine no amount of marketing spend can replicate. The formal organization name is ISITO. The treaty framework is The Solaris Accord. The station itself belongs to the world.

6-week open global name submission in 12+ languages. No restrictions on creativity. Target: millions of submissions, massive press cycle #1.

Shortlist Curation

ISITO linguistic panel narrows to 5–7 finalists. Requirements: translatable without negative connotations in major world languages, technically appropriate, not trademarked.

Global Public Vote

4-week vote in all major languages. Open globally with no eligibility restrictions. One vote per verified identity. Press cycle #2.

Naming Ceremony

Globally broadcast announcement event. The moment ISITO becomes real to the world. Press cycle #3 — this is your public launch. The station has a name before it has a module.

Every risk identified below was stress-tested through the design process. None are deal-breakers. All have mitigation paths. Investors should see this register as evidence of engineering rigor, not cause for alarm.
